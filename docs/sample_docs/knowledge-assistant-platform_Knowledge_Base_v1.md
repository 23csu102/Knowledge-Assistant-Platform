## 1. Overview & Background
The National Mission for Clean Ganga (NMCG) is the flagship implementation body of the Government of India for the rejuvenation, protection, and sustainable management of the Ganga River — India's national river. Spanning more than 2,525 kilometres from Gaumukh in Uttarakhand to the Bay of Bengal at Ganga Sagar in West Bengal, the Ganga basin covers roughly 26% of India's landmass, supports over 500 million people, and generates more than 40% of India's GDP.
The Ganga has faced severe ecological stress from decades of untreated municipal sewage discharge, industrial effluents, agricultural runoff, encroachment of floodplains, and unsustainable water abstraction. By the early 2000s, vast stretches of the river had deteriorated to levels unsafe for bathing or drinking, placing enormous pressure on public health, biodiversity, and India's cultural heritage.
The NMCG was established as a coordinated, basin-wide institutional response to this crisis — unifying engineering interventions, biodiversity conservation, community participation, and economic development under a single umbrella framework.



## 2. Historical Background & Evolution
### 2.1 Pre-NMCG Efforts: Ganga Action Plan Era
India's formal efforts to clean the Ganga date to 1985, when the Ganga Action Plan (GAP) Phase I was launched under Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi. Over the next two decades, an estimated Rs. 1,000 crore was spent on GAP Phase I and II, yet surveys by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) consistently showed no sustained improvement in water quality. These earlier programs suffered from fragmented implementation, inadequate monitoring, absence of community involvement, and a purely engineering-centric approach that ignored the river's ecological flow.
In 2009, the National Ganga River Basin Authority (NGRBA) was constituted under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, marking a shift toward a basin-wide, inter-state approach. The NGRBA launched the Mission Clean Ganga with objectives that included effective abatement of pollution and maintaining minimum ecological flows. However, the NGRBA structure was later replaced by a more empowered framework.
### 2.2 Establishment of NMCG (2011)
On 12 August 2011, the National Mission for Clean Ganga was registered as a society under the Societies Registration Act, 1860. It initially functioned as the implementation arm of the NGRBA, coordinating activities across the five Ganga basin states. The NMCG's formation signalled a recognition that cleaning the Ganga required not just pollution abatement, but comprehensive ecological restoration — encompassing e-flows, biodiversity, forestry, and public participation.
### 2.3 Namami Gange Programme (2014)
The Namami Gange Programme — translating from Sanskrit as 'Obeisance to the Ganga' — was launched in June 2014 as a flagship programme of the Union Government with an initial budget of Rs. 20,000 crore. Approved by the Union Cabinet in May 2015, it combined immediate visible activities, medium-term pollution control, and long-term ecological restoration into a structured, phased programme. The budget was five times larger than all combined spending on similar efforts since 1985.
### 2.4 Statutory Elevation (2016)
A landmark moment came on 7 October 2016, when the National Ganga Council was constituted under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, through Notification S.O. 3187(E), dissolving the NGRBA. The NMCG was elevated to a statutory authority, and a five-tier national-state-district governance structure was established. The National Ganga Council is chaired by the Prime Minister of India, with Chief Ministers of the five basin states and several Union Ministers as members.
### 2.5 Namami Gange 2.0 (2021–2026)
The programme was extended beyond its original March 2021 deadline to March 2026, with an enhanced outlay of Rs. 22,500 crore (bringing the cumulative budget to Rs. 42,500 crore). This phase — informally called Namami Gange 2.0 — focused on completing pending sewage treatment infrastructure, expanding biodiversity programmes, and launching the Arth Ganga initiative linking river rejuvenation with sustainable economic livelihoods.

## 4. Organisational Structure & Governance
The NMCG operates through a multi-tiered governance architecture established under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986. This five-tier structure ensures coordination from the national level down to individual districts, bringing all stakeholders onto a single platform.
### 4.1 Five-Tier Governance Structure
### Tier 1 — National Ganga Council (NGC):
Chaired by the Prime Minister of India. Includes Chief Ministers of the five Ganga basin states and several Union Ministers. Sets policy direction and ensures political will for Ganga rejuvenation. Reviews progress at the highest level of government.
### Tier 2 — Empowered Task Force (ETF):
Chaired by the Union Minister of Jal Shakti (Department of Water Resources, River Development & Ganga Rejuvenation). Oversees implementation and resolves inter-ministerial and inter-state coordination issues.
### Tier 3 — National Mission for Clean Ganga (NMCG):
The operational core. Has a two-tier internal structure: (a) Governing Council headed by the Director General, NMCG; and (b) Executive Committee, also headed by the Director General, which can sanction individual projects up to Rs. 1,000 crore.
### Tier 4 — State Ganga Committees:
One in each of the five basin states. Supported by State Programme Management Groups (SPMGs) headed by senior state officers, which serve as implementing arms analogous to NMCG at the state level.
### Tier 5 — District Ganga Committees:
Constituted in every specified district abutting the Ganga and its tributaries. Ensures last-mile implementation, community engagement, and local accountability.
### 4.2 Key Institutional Partners
Seven IITs collaborated in preparing the Ganga River Basin Management Plan (GRBMP); 13 IITs adopted 65 villages as model villages.: IIT Consortium
Established 2016; serves as NMCG's think tank and data collection centre.: cGanga (Centre for Ganga River Basin Management and Studies)

## 6. Eight Pillars of the Namami Gange Programme
The Namami Gange Programme is structured around eight fundamental pillars that collectively address pollution, ecology, community participation, and long-term sustainability:
### Pillar 1 — Sewage Treatment Infrastructure
The largest pillar by financial outlay (~80% of total budget). Involves construction, rehabilitation, and modernisation of Sewage Treatment Plants (STPs) and sewerage networks. The Hybrid Annuity Model (HAM) and Design-Build-Operate-Transfer (DBOT) frameworks are used to attract private sector investment and ensure 17-year operational maintenance cycles.
### Pillar 2 — Industrial Effluent Monitoring
Real-time monitoring of grossly polluting industries (GPIs) through SCADA systems and automatic effluent monitoring devices. Industries found non-compliant with discharge standards face closure orders. The Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD) load from GPIs along the Ganga declined from 26 TPD in 2017 to 13.73 TPD in 2023 — a 47% reduction.
### Pillar 3 — River Surface Cleaning
Deployed at 11 locations along the river for collection and disposal of floating solid waste from the surface of ghats and the river. Uses robotic trash skimmers, mechanical cleaning boats, and solid waste management systems.
### Pillar 4 — River Front Development
Renovation, modernisation, and construction of ghats (bathing steps) and crematoria (electric/CNG crematoriums replacing open-air wood-burning) to reduce unburnt human remains and ritual waste entering the river, while improving the human-river connect.
### Pillar 5 — Biodiversity Conservation
Science-based restoration of aquatic and riparian species. The Ganga basin hosts approximately 2,000 aquatic species including 143 fish species, 90 amphibian species, the critically endangered Gangetic dolphin (Platanista gangetica), three otter species, gharial, mugger crocodile, and 12 freshwater turtle species. NMCG funds the Wildlife Institute of India, CIFRI, and state forest departments for species conservation programmes.
### Pillar 6 — Afforestation
The Forestry Interventions for Ganga project has been implemented since 2016-17 by State Forest Departments of Uttarakhand, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Jharkhand, and West Bengal. NMCG provides financial support to state forest departments for riparian and watershed afforestation.
### Pillar 7 — Public Awareness & Ganga Gram
A comprehensive community engagement programme covering events, workshops, seminars, rallies, cleanliness drives, plantation drives, TV/radio campaigns, print media, and school programmes. Ganga Gram initiatives have adopted villages along the river to demonstrate model sanitation, waste management, and conservation practices.
### Pillar 8 — Arth Ganga (Economic Connect)
Launched in 2019, Arth Ganga links river rejuvenation to sustainable livelihoods through five sub-pillars: Natural Farming (reducing chemical runoff), Treated Water Reuse, Livelihood Generation through Self-Help Groups, Cultural Heritage Promotion, and Eco-Tourism. Jalaj Centres serve as hubs for community income through dolphin safaris, homestays, and eco-tourism.

## 7. Key Achievements & Progress
### 7.1 Statistical Highlights (as of 2026)



### 7.2 Sewage Treatment Infrastructure
Sewage treatment lies at the heart of the Namami Gange Programme. Historically, over 6 billion litres of sewage were released daily into the Ganga and its tributaries without treatment.
193 sewerage infrastructure projects sanctioned, targeting creation and rehabilitation of 6,029.75 MLD of STP capacity and approximately 5,250.98 km of sewerage network.
Sewage treatment capacity surged to 3,446 MLD — a 30-fold increase compared to pre-2014 levels.
During FY 2025-26 alone, 538.03 MLD of new capacity was added through 18 projects across five states, with 28 STPs completed.
These projects represent investment of approximately Rs. 4,700 crore in FY 2025-26 alone.
Target: 7,000 MLD cumulative treatment capacity by December 2026.
The Hybrid Annuity Model ensures STPs are not just built but maintained for 17 years, addressing the operational failure that plagued earlier programmes.
Ganga Pulse Public Portal launched to display real-time STP performance data (pH, BOD, TSS) for all five basin states, ensuring transparency and accountability.
### 7.3 Biodiversity & Ecological Recovery
Gangetic Dolphin Population: The dolphin population — a key indicator species for river health — grew from approximately 3,000 in 2009 to 3,330 (±630) in 2018 to 3,936 (±763) in 2024. The 2021-23 census recorded approximately 6,327 individuals. Dolphins have been spotted in previously unrecorded locations, indicating improved aquatic health across multiple regions.
Fish Ranching (2024): 49.25 lakh Indian Major Carps, 7,370 Mahseer, 42,117 Hilsa, and 1,387 tagged Hilsa were released as part of NMCG-CIFRI fish ranching programmes.
Dolphin Ambulance: A specialised rescue vehicle approved to assist stranded Gangetic dolphins, alongside community training in dolphin conservation.
National Dolphin Research Centre (NDRC): Being established at Patna University on a 4,400 sq metre plot for research and conservation.
Vikramshila Ganges Dolphin Sanctuary: Established in Bihar as a dedicated dolphin protected area.
Turtle Conservation: Projects approved for rehabilitation and reintroduction of three critically threatened turtle species in Uttar Pradesh's Ganga basin.
Gharial: Though populations remain critically low (~182 breeding adults), gharial conservation is integrated into NMCG's biodiversity action plan.
National Ganga River Dolphin Day: Celebrated on 5 October annually to raise public awareness.
### 7.4 Water Quality Improvements
CPCB data shows measurable improvements in BOD levels across key river stretches, particularly in Haridwar, Rishikesh, Kanpur, Varanasi, and Prayagraj.
During the COVID-19 lockdown (2020), water quality in the Hooghly River showed dramatic short-term improvements: Cadmium (Cd) down 50%, Lead (Pb) down 53%, Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) down 62%, BOD down 52% — demonstrating the river's potential to recover when pollution inputs reduce.
BOD load from grossly polluting industries along the Ganga declined from 26 TPD in 2017 to 13.73 TPD in 2023 (47% reduction).
Real-time SCADA systems and automatic effluent monitors are now deployed at key industrial units along the river.
### 7.5 International Recognition

2022: International Water Association (IWA) honoured NMCG as a Climate Smart Utility.
2021: Clean Ganga Roadshow launched at COP26 in Glasgow with four international chapters (Scotland, Wales, Midlands, London).
Active bilateral MoUs with Germany (GIZ), Denmark, Israel, UK, Australia, Finland, and UNDP for technology transfer and capacity building.
### 7.6 Urban River Management
River Cities Alliance (RCA): Launched November 2021, expanded from 30 to 145 cities across India. NIUA serves as Secretariat. Provides capacity building, knowledge exchange, and mentoring for city-level river management.
Urban River Management Plans (URMPs): Developed for Kanpur, Ayodhya, Chatrapati Sambhaji Nagar, Bareilly, Moradabad, and Delhi (in progress with NIUA).
District Ganga Plans (DGPs): Inception workshop held July 2024 in association with GIZ; four pilot districts selected for comprehensive river basin management plans.
### 7.7 Financial Progress
Total funds released to NMCG from FY 2014-15 to FY 2023-24: Rs. 20,424.82 crore.
NMCG has disbursed Rs. 16,648.49 crore (82% of allocation) to implementing agencies.
Finance Ministry granted income tax exemption to NMCG from assessment year 2024-25 (April 2025), recognising it as an authority under the EPA 1986.

## 8. Implementation Phases
### 8.1 Entry-Level Activities (Immediate Impact)
River surface cleaning at ghats and on the river.
Improvement of rural sanitation and construction of toilets under Swachh Bharat Mission linkages.
Renovation, modernisation, and new construction of ghats.
Renovation, modernisation, and construction of electric/CNG crematoria.
### 8.2 Medium-Term Activities (Within 5 Years)
Interception and diversion of sewage drains; construction and rehabilitation of STPs.
Hybrid Annuity Model-based PPP for sewage treatment infrastructure.
Real-time monitoring of industrial effluent discharge.
Preparation of guidelines on best available technologies for pollution treatment.
Urban River Management Plans for river cities.
### 8.3 Long-Term Activities (Within 10 Years)
Ensuring minimum ecological flows (e-flows) in the Ganga and all major tributaries.
Restoration of viable populations of all endemic and endangered aquatic species across their historical range.
Forestry interventions: afforestation of riparian buffers and catchment areas.
Tributary rejuvenation: the Namami Niranjana Abhiyan (Feb 2024) targets the Niranjana/Falgu river system in Jharkhand/Bihar.
Basin-level sediment management under the National Framework for Sediment Management (2022).

## 9. Use Cases & Case Studies
### 9.1 Varanasi — Integrated Sewage Management
Varanasi (Banaras), one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities and a major pilgrimage site, represents one of NMCG's flagship urban interventions. Multiple STPs have been developed, including a major project in the Assi-BHU area adding 55 MLD capacity. The Dinapur STP complex has been upgraded and expanded. The One-City-One-Operator model creates unified accountability for wastewater treatment across the city, ensuring consistent operational performance. Ghat renovation along the Dashashwamedh, Assi, and Manikarnika ghats has improved riverfront aesthetics and reduced ritual waste.
### 9.2 Kanpur — Industrial Effluent Control
Kanpur, home to India's largest leather tannery industry, was historically one of the most critical pollution hotspots on the Ganga. Chromium and other heavy metal discharges from tanneries severely degraded water quality. NMCG's interventions included installation of real-time SCADA monitoring on tannery effluent outlets, stringent enforcement by the National Green Tribunal, common effluent treatment plants for tannery clusters, and interception of sewage drains. Kanpur also became one of the first cities to develop an Urban River Management Plan under the NMCG-NIUA framework.
### 9.3 Haridwar & Rishikesh — Pilgrimage City Restoration
These two cities at the Ganga's foothills represent critical upstream intervention points. The Jagjeetpur STP in Haridwar, one of the larger STPs on the river, was rehabilitated and expanded. Multiple decentralised interventions were implemented across Haridwar — Jagjeetpur, Sarai, Rishikesh, Srinagar, and Devprayag — collectively adding 11.23 MLD capacity during FY 2025-26. During the Mahakumbh Mela, NMCG co-ordinates with state agencies to deploy temporary sanitation facilities and monitor water quality in real time at bathing ghats.
### 9.4 Vikramshila Dolphin Sanctuary — Bihar
The Vikramshila Ganges Dolphin Sanctuary in Bihar covers a 50 km stretch of the Ganga near Bhagalpur. It represents India's first protected area specifically for the Gangetic dolphin. NMCG supports the sanctuary with funding for research, community-based monitoring, eco-tourism through Jalaj Centres, and the 'Dolphin Mitra' community volunteer programme. The sanctuary demonstrates the model of linking biodiversity conservation with local livelihood generation.
### 9.5 Prayagraj — Kumbh Mela Water Quality Management
Prayagraj hosts the world's largest human gathering — the Kumbh Mela — at the Triveni Sangam (confluence of Ganga, Yamuna, and Saraswati). For the 2019 Kumbh and the 2025 Mahakumbh, NMCG undertook major pre-event pollution control measures including interception and diversion of 13 drains (adding 43 MLD STP capacity at Salori at a sanctioned cost of Rs. 331.75 crore), deployment of trash skimmers and floating cleanup vessels, and real-time water quality monitoring at bathing ghats. For Mahakumbh 2025, Rs. 1,062 crore in projects were sanctioned at the 57th EC meeting.
### 9.6 Arth Ganga — Garhmukteshwar Model
In Garhmukteshwar (Uttar Pradesh), the Arth Ganga framework was tested as a model for linking river conservation with community livelihoods. Self-Help Groups were trained in organic/natural farming to reduce chemical runoff. Jalaj Centres were set up for eco-tourism and dolphin safari operations. Treated wastewater was redirected for agricultural use, demonstrating the circular economy model for river water. This model is now being replicated across the Ganga basin.
### 9.7 Smart Laboratory for Clean Rivers (SLCR) — Indo-Danish Partnership
Established under the Green Strategic Partnership between India and Denmark, the Smart Laboratory for Clean Rivers brings global technology solutions to India's river cleaning challenges. It operates through a Living Lab approach, combining government authorities, academic institutions, and technology providers in co-creation mode. Research focuses on advanced water treatment technologies, real-time monitoring systems, and community water management innovations — with an aim to transfer successful solutions into mainstream NMCG projects.
### 9.8 Ganga Gram — Model Village Programme
Under the Ganga Gram programme, villages located along the Ganga's banks are transformed into model villages for sanitation, solid waste management, and community participation in river conservation. IIT consortium members adopted 65 villages as model villages, implementing end-to-end waste management, liquid waste treatment, and participatory planning processes that can be replicated across all Ganga basin villages.

## 10. Challenges & Criticisms
### 10.1 Incomplete Sewage Network Coverage
A significant portion of India's urban population lives outside sewerage networks, resulting in large volumes of domestic wastewater bypassing STPs entirely. Even where STPs exist, collection infrastructure is often inadequate, leading to underutilisation of treatment capacity. CPCB (2023) data indicates that 71% of monitoring stations still exceed faecal coliform limits, reflecting the scale of the remaining challenge.
### 10.2 Municipal Solid Waste
The Quality Council of India found rubbish heaps near ghats in numerous towns along the river, indicating persistent improper solid waste disposal. Many towns lack proper landfill or waste treatment infrastructure, allowing solid waste to enter the river — a problem the mission has been criticised for not adequately addressing in early phases.
### 10.3 Budget Utilisation Gaps
As of June 2024, approximately Rs. 37,550 crore had been allocated to Namami Gange projects, but only Rs. 18,033 crore had been utilised. While the gap partly reflects the annuity-based payment structure for HAM projects, it has drawn scrutiny from the Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG) and civil society groups regarding implementation pace.
### 10.4 Industrial Compliance
Despite SCADA monitoring and closure orders, 35 out of 764 grossly polluting industries along the Ganga remain non-compliant as of 2023. Enforcement challenges persist due to the economic and political influence of industry, especially in smaller cities lacking strong local governance.
### 10.5 Hydrological Deterioration
Research indicates that in many stretches of the Ganga, up to 60% of natural flow is abstracted for irrigation and other uses. Climate change is exacerbating the situation, with baseflows declining by approximately 17% per decade. Desilting for navigation and upstream dam operations further alter the river's natural hydrological regime. Basin-level water governance reforms remain critical but politically complex.
### 10.6 Microplastics & Emerging Contaminants
Recent scientific studies have documented microplastics and nanomaterials infiltrating Ganga aquifers. Emerging organic contaminants from pharmaceutical and personal care product manufacturing — particularly in the Kanpur-Unnao industrial belt — pose risks not addressed by conventional STPs. Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes), a pollution-tolerant invasive species, facilitates pathogenic bacteria including Vibrio cholerae and Aeromonas, creating secondary public health risks.
### 10.7 Governance Coordination
The five-tier institutional structure, while comprehensive, sometimes results in coordination delays between national, state, and district levels. State-level SPMGs vary significantly in capacity and political commitment. Inter-state water sharing disputes and industrial lobbying can slow environmental enforcement.

## 12. Recent Developments (2024–2026)
### 12.1 Mahakumbh 2025 Preparation
The 57th Executive Committee meeting of NMCG sanctioned projects worth Rs. 1,062 crore for Mahakumbh 2025, the world's largest religious gathering at Prayagraj. Projects included interception and diversion of sewage drains in Prayagraj, installation of temporary STPs, real-time water quality monitoring at bathing ghats, and comprehensive IEC activities. Mahakumbh 2025 drew an estimated 400-600 million visitors between January and February 2025.
### 12.2 Ganga Pulse Portal
NMCG developed the Ganga Pulse Public Portal as a monitoring tool displaying real-time data from all STPs across the five Ganga basin states. The portal shows pH, BOD, and TSS levels at both inlet and outlet points, enabling public verification of treatment efficiency. This transparency mechanism addresses long-standing concerns about STP operational performance and represents a significant step in citizen accountability.
### 12.3 Namami Niranjana Abhiyan
Launched on 20 February 2024, the Namami Niranjana Abhiyan targets the Niranjana (Falgu) river — the sacred river at Bodh Gaya (where the Buddha attained enlightenment) and Gaya (a major Hindu pilgrimage site for ancestral rituals). The initiative aims to ensure the river's perennial flow and is part of NMCG's expanded mandate to rejuvenate major tributaries beyond the main Ganga stem.
### 12.4 Smart Laboratory for Clean Rivers (SLCR)
Established under the India-Denmark Green Strategic Partnership, the SLCR brings cutting-edge water treatment technologies to real-world river conditions through a Living Lab methodology. The first meeting of the India-Israel Joint Review Committee (JRC) under their bilateral MoU was held in October 2024, broadening the international technology network around NMCG.
### 12.5 FY 2025-26 Infrastructure Milestones
The most recent reporting period (FY 2025-26) saw NMCG add 538.03 MLD of treatment capacity through 18 projects across UP, Uttarakhand, Jharkhand, West Bengal, and Bihar. Key projects included: Varanasi (Assi-BHU area, 55 MLD), Agra (166 MLD), Prayagraj (Salori STP augmentation, 43 MLD), Moradabad (Ramganga pollution abatement, 25 MLD), and Vrindavan (I&D and STP works, 13 MLD). Twenty-eight STPs were completed — surpassing the 22 completed the previous year.

## 13. Way Forward & Future Outlook
### 13.1 Namami Gange 3.0
With the current programme extended until March 2026, policy discussions have begun on a third phase (Namami Gange 3.0) that will likely focus on: (a) transitioning from infrastructure creation to operational excellence and quality assurance; (b) scaling up the Arth Ganga circular economy model; (c) comprehensive tributary rejuvenation across all major Ganga tributaries; (d) climate resilience through enhanced e-flow maintenance and watershed management; and (e) district-level planning through the District Ganga Plans being piloted with GIZ support.
### 13.2 Technology & Innovation Agenda
The Smart Laboratory for Clean Rivers, combined with AI-driven SCADA monitoring and the Ganga Pulse Portal, points toward a data-driven future for river management. Key technology priorities include advanced treatment of emerging contaminants (pharmaceuticals, microplastics), nature-based solutions for riverfront management, precision monitoring using satellite remote sensing, and digital twin modelling of the river basin for scenario planning.
### 13.3 Scaling the River Cities Alliance
With 145 cities now members of the RCA, the focus is shifting to actual URMP implementation. Cities like Kanpur, Ayodhya, Bareilly, and Moradabad have developed city-specific river management plans; the challenge is securing funding and building institutional capacity to implement them effectively.
### 13.4 Community & Culture
The Arth Ganga initiative's emphasis on linking livelihoods to river health represents the most promising pathway to sustainable community participation. Scaling Jalaj Centres, expanding dolphin safari eco-tourism, and creating market linkages for natural farming produce along the Ganga basin could create durable economic incentives for river conservation that outlast government programme cycles.

2015 | Union Cabinet formally approves Namami Gange Programme (May 2015). National Ganga Council constituted with PM as chair. Programme outlay set at 5x the previous three decades of spending.
2016 | NMCG elevated to statutory authority under EPA 1986 (October 2016). NGRBA dissolved. Centre for Ganga River Basin Management and Studies (cGanga) established as think tank. National Green Tribunal prohibits waste disposal in Ganga.
2017 | Hybrid Annuity Model (HAM) introduced for sewage treatment plants — private developers share financial risk. Forestry Interventions for Ganga project begins across five states.
2018 | River Cities Alliance framework conceived. Wildlife Institute of India dolphin census records approximately 3,330 dolphins.
2019 | Arth Ganga initiative approved as inter-ministerial programme linking river economy to conservation. Ministry of Jal Shakti formed by merging water-related departments.
2020 | Urban River Management Plan (URMP) framework launched (Nov 2020) by NIUA in partnership with NMCG.
2021 | River Cities Alliance (RCA) formally launched (Nov 2021) — initially 30 cities, expanded to 145 cities. UN DECADE on Ecosystem Restoration programme launched. Ganga dolphin population in NMCG data: 147 of 341 projects completed.
2022 | UN Environment Programme names Namami Gange one of ten World Restoration Flagship initiatives. International Water Association honours NMCG as Climate Smart Utility. National Framework for Sediment Management published.
2023 | Total of 442 projects sanctioned, 254 completed. Tripartite Concession Agreement signed for 60 MLD STP at Gokul Barrage, Mathura. BOD load from grossly polluting industries declines 47% since 2017.
2024 | NMCG launches Namami Niranjana Abhiyan (Feb 2024). Dolphin population rises to 3,936. 8th India Water Week held. 8th Ganga Utsav at Haridwar. Smart Laboratory for Clean Rivers (SLCR) established with Denmark.
2025 | Finance Ministry grants income tax exemption to NMCG (April 2025). 57th EC meeting sanctions Rs. 1,062 crore for conservation and Mahakumbh 2025 IEC activities. Dolphin population census records approximately 6,327 individuals.
2026 | FY 2025-26 adds 538.03 MLD treatment capacity through 18 new projects. 28 STPs completed during the year. Programme targeted to achieve 7,000 MLD cumulative treatment capacity by December 2026.
